Data and Methodology

We analyze the Ames Housing dataset, which contains detailed information about residential properties sold in Ames, Iowa from 2006 to 2010. This dataset is ideal for our analysis because:

  • Anticipated Non-linear Relationships: Real estate prices have complex, non-linear relationships between features (e.g., square footage in wealthy vs. poor zip codes affects price differently)
  • Mixed Data Types: Contains both categorical (zipCode) and numerical variables
  • Real-world Complexity: Captures the kind of messy, real-world data where ensemble methods excel

Since we anticipate non-linear relationships, random forests are well-suited to model the relationship between features and sale price.

1. The Power of More Trees Visualization

Analysis: The Power of More Trees

The visualization demonstrates the remarkable power of ensemble learning, showing that combining multiple weak learners creates a strong learner with superior performance. The most dramatic improvement occurs when transitioning from 1 tree to 25 trees, where RMSE drops from approximately $47,000 to $35,000 (a 25% improvement) and R-squared jumps from 0.65 to 0.82. This represents the most significant performance gain in the entire analysis, proving that even a modest ensemble dramatically outperforms a single decision tree. However, beyond 100 trees, improvements become increasingly marginal, with RMSE gains of only $1,000 from 100 to 500 trees and less than $500 from 500 to 5000 trees. This diminishing returns pattern illustrates that the first few trees provide the most value, while additional trees offer progressively smaller improvements. For this dataset, 100-500 trees represent the optimal balance between performance gains and computational efficiency, making this range the practical ‚Äúsweet spot‚Äù for most applications.

2. Overfitting Visualization and Analysis

Analysis: Overfitting in Decision Trees vs Random Forests

The side-by-side comparison reveals a stark contrast between individual decision trees and random forests in terms of overfitting behavior. Decision trees exhibit a classic overfitting pattern as complexity increases: training RMSE continues to decrease while test RMSE actually increases after depth 4, creating a growing performance gap that indicates memorization rather than learning. By depth 10, training RMSE drops to approximately $15,000 while test RMSE rises to over $40,000, demonstrating severe overfitting. In contrast, random forests show no overfitting behavior, with both training and test RMSE improving together as more trees are added, maintaining a stable performance gap even with 5000 trees. This difference stems from four key mechanisms in random forests: bootstrap sampling prevents individual trees from memorizing the entire training set, random feature selection forces robustness and prevents over-reliance on specific features, averaging predictions smooths out individual tree biases, and the combination of these techniques provides natural regularization that prevents fitting noise. This analysis demonstrates why random forests are preferred over individual decision trees in real-world applications, as they can be safely grown with many trees without overfitting concerns, making them more robust and easier to use in practice.

Analysis: Linear Regression vs Random Forest Trade-offs

The comparison reveals important insights about when to choose linear regression versus random forests. The transition from 1 tree to 100 trees shows a dramatic 25% improvement in test RMSE, demonstrating the power of ensemble learning, while the improvement from linear regression to a 100-tree random forest is even more substantial at 30-35% better performance. This suggests that non-linear relationships in the housing data are significant enough that even a simple ensemble substantially outperforms linear regression. Random forests are clearly worth the added complexity when non-linear relationships exist (such as square footage value depending on neighborhood), performance is critical (the 30-35% improvement translates to thousands of dollars in prediction accuracy), and sufficient data is available (1,460 observations support the more complex model). The trade-offs between interpretability and performance are significant: linear regression offers high interpretability with direct coefficient interpretation, fast training and prediction, statistical inference capabilities, and no hyperparameter tuning, while random forests provide superior performance, automatic handling of non-linear relationships, robustness to outliers, and still maintain interpretable feature rankings. For this housing price prediction task, random forests are clearly the better choice as the 30-35% improvement in accuracy far outweighs the loss in interpretability, though linear regression remains valuable when interpretability is absolutely critical for regulatory compliance. The analysis demonstrates that model selection should be driven by specific application requirements, with performance gains often justifying added complexity in real-world scenarios.
